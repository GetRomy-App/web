  ## 1. Personal and Professional Background
  
  Robert Frederick Smith was born December 1, 1962 in Denver, Colorado, to two parents who both held PhDs in education — his father an elementary school principal, his mother the principal of George Washington High School. He attended East High School in Denver and famously persisted in landing a Bell Labs internship that had been intended only for college students while he was still in high school.
  
  He earned a B.S. in chemical engineering from Cornell University in 1985, then worked at Goodyear Tire and Rubber and at Kraft General Foods, where he obtained two U.S. and two European patents for coffee filtration systems. He completed his MBA at Columbia Business School in 1994 and joined Goldman Sachs, where as Co-Head of Enterprise Systems and Storage he advised on more than $50 billion in M&A activity involving companies including Apple, Microsoft, Texas Instruments, eBay and Yahoo, and was the first banker in Goldman's San Francisco office focused exclusively on technology and software M&A.
  
  Smith founded Vista Equity Partners in Austin, Texas in 2000. Vista today manages over $107 billion in equity assets and oversees a portfolio of more than 90 enterprise software and data companies employing over 100,000 people worldwide; Smith has supervised more than 650 transactions representing over $350 billion in aggregate value since founding. As of 2019, Vista was the fourth-largest enterprise software entity globally after Microsoft, Oracle, and SAP when measured across all holdings.
  
  Personal life: He was married to Suzanne McFayden from 1988 to 2014, and in 2015 married Hope Dworaczyk, founder/CEO of skincare company MUTHA; he has eight children across both marriages and maintains residences in Austin, Malibu, Manhattan, North Palm Beach, and Denver, per Goodreturns' biographical compilation.
  
  Recognitions of relevance to a Texas pitch: inducted into the Texas Business Hall of Fame in 2019, named to TIME100 in 2020, awarded the Carnegie Medal of Philanthropy in 2019, and recognized as one of Forbes' 100 Greatest Living Business Minds in 2017. In 2025 he was named to TIME100's "Most Influential People in Philanthropy".

  **Realistic capacity bands for a single gift to a mid-sized D.C. journalism/media literacy nonprofit:**
  
  - **Entry / first-time gift:** $250K – $750K (one year, project-specific)
  - **Established multi-year relationship:** $1M – $3M over 2–3 years
  - **Strategic anchor commitment** (if the org becomes part of her core media portfolio): $5M – $10M+ multi-year
  
  For context, a $1M ask is squarely within her "low six figures to low seven figures" pattern for journalism nonprofits and would not be an outlier. A $5M+ ask would require alignment as a flagship investment in her media portfolio, akin to her ProPublica or American Journalism Project posture.
  
  ## 6. Recommended Ask Amount and Engagement Strategy
  
  ### Recommended ask
  **$1.5M over three years** (approximately $500K/year), framed as a capacity-building grant tied to a specific, measurable program — ideally one that bridges *journalism* and *media literacy* (an emerging gap in the field she funds, given her current concentration in production-side journalism rather than audience-side trust and information ecosystem work).
  
  ### Engagement strategy
  
  **Phase 1 — Warm introduction (months 0–3):** Do not cold-pitch Emerson. The most viable introduction routes are:
  - **Evan Smith** (Senior Advisor, Emerson Collective; former Texas Tribune CEO) — most directly accessible journalism gateway
  - **Anne Marie Burgoyne** (Managing Director of Social Innovation and Impact)
  - **Ford Foundation** trustees and program staff (she sits on the board)
  - The **American Journalism Project** ecosystem — if your nonprofit is or could be in their portfolio
  - Editorial leadership at **The Atlantic** for thematic alignment, not as a funding pathway
  
  **Phase 2 — Concept positioning:** Frame the proposal around three themes that consistently animate her giving — democracy as a fragile system, leaders as the asset, and the local/civic information ecosystem. Media literacy should be positioned as the *demand-side complement* to her supply-side investments in nonprofit newsrooms (i.e., journalism cannot save democracy if audiences cannot evaluate what they read).
  
  **Phase 3 — Cultivation:** Expect a long courtship. Emerson tends to invest after observing leaders for months or years. Build relationship without immediate ask — invite to convenings, share substantive memos on the field, connect their grantees to your work. Press Forward affiliation (the national local-news collaborative) is a positive signal.
  
  **Phase 4 — Ask:** A multi-year, leader-led, capacity-building proposal with a clear measurement framework. Avoid restricted single-program asks. Emphasize organizational sustainability, not project deliverables.
  
  **Phase 5 — Stewardship:** Anticipate an anonymous gift posture. Do not plan a named building, named program, or press release — this is a deal-breaker for most Emerson grants.
  
  ## 7. Key Connection Points and Potential Red Flags
  
  ### Connection points / warm vectors
  - **D.C. presence:** Emerson Collective has a Washington office, lowering geographic friction
  - **Ford Foundation:** Shared funder ecosystem; possible introductions through Darren Walker's network
  - **Council on Foreign Relations:** Useful for D.C. policy circles
  - **Stanford and University of Pennsylvania:** alumni connections
  - **Press Forward / American Journalism Project:** if the prospect is already in or aligned with these collaboratives, the conceptual sale is half-done
  - **The Atlantic** (D.C. headquartered): editorial relationships are *adjacent* to her funding priorities and can build institutional familiarity
  
  ### Red flags and risk factors
  
  **Transparency risk:** In 2019, Inside Philanthropy designated Powell Jobs the "Least Transparent Mega-Giver", and Emerson's LLC structure means no public 990 disclosures. This complicates donor reporting, mutual due diligence, and prospect tracking — and means most insights into giving size come from grantee disclosures, not Emerson's books.
  
  **Editorial independence concerns:** Business Insider reporting in 2024 alleged that Emerson cut funding to Chalkbeat following critical coverage of XQ in 2019, though both organizations have publicly disputed the framing. For a journalism/media literacy nonprofit, this creates a real reputational tension — accepting major funding from Powell Jobs requires explicit editorial firewalls and likely a board-level conflict-of-interest policy. The optics matter especially for a media literacy organization whose credibility depends on appearing nonaligned.
  
  **Political alignment risk:** Powell Jobs is a major Democratic donor. For a media literacy nonprofit serving audiences across the political spectrum, this could complicate trust-building with conservative-leaning audiences if the funding becomes a salient public fact. Consider how funding would be disclosed and whether matching funds from politically diverse sources should be sought to balance perception.
  
  **Competitive overlap:** She owns The Atlantic and is invested in Axios, The Athletic, and Pushkin. A media literacy nonprofit that could be perceived as competing with or critiquing Atlantic-adjacent properties may face cooler reception.
  
  **Access difficulty:** Emerson is famously hard to reach without warm intros. Direct outreach via website forms is reportedly low-yield. Inside Philanthropy describes Emerson Collective as not a likely source of funding for most grant seekers.
  
  **Strategic shifts:** Powell Jobs is in her early sixties with an explicit spend-down posture. Climate (Waverley Street, $3.5B) is now her largest commitment and may be drawing attention away from journalism. Confirm current journalism portfolio appetite before extensive cultivation.

  ### Bottom line
  
  Powell Jobs is an **A-tier prospect** for a D.C. journalism/media literacy nonprofit — capacity is essentially unconstrained, cause alignment is strong, and the geographic fit is favorable. The bottleneck is **access and positioning**, not capacity or interest. Success depends almost entirely on a warm introduction (Evan Smith or Anne Marie Burgoyne are the highest-leverage points), a leader-driven narrative, a multi-year capacity-building structure, and willingness to accept anonymous, lightly-stewarded giving. Plan for a 12–24 month cultivation cycle before a meaningful ask.
